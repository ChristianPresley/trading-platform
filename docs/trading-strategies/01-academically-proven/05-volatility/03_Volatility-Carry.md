# Volatility Carry

> **Source**: [151 Trading Strategies](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3247865) Ch. 7, [Sepp (2018) — Lessons from the Crash of Short Volatility ETFs](https://artursepp.com/2018/02/15/lessons-from-the-crash-of-short-volatility-etfs/)
> **Asset Class**: Volatility ETPs / Futures
> **Crypto/24-7 Applicable**: No — requires a liquid VIX futures market and volatility ETPs; no equivalent instruments exist in crypto with sufficient depth
> **Evidence Tier**: Backtested Only
> **Complexity**: Simple

## Overview

Volatility carry is the strategy of systematically holding short positions in volatility exchange-traded notes (ETNs) and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) to capture the "carry" generated by the persistent contango in VIX futures. This carry arises because long volatility products (VXX, UVXY) must continuously roll their futures exposure from cheaper near-term contracts into more expensive longer-term contracts during contango, creating a structural drag that benefits short sellers.

The mechanism is related to the VIX futures basis strategy but implemented through the ETN/ETF wrapper, which simplifies execution at the cost of introducing product-specific risks (tracking error, issuer risk, path dependency). The short volatility ETN approach gained popularity in the 2012-2018 period, when XIV (VelocityShares Daily Inverse VIX Short-Term Futures ETN) and SVXY (ProShares Short VIX Short-Term Futures ETF) attracted billions in assets and delivered annualized returns exceeding 50% in some years.

Academic analysis (Sepp, 2018) showed that short volatility ETNs provide leveraged beta exposure to the S&P 500 with an effective leverage ratio of approximately 4.2x — they do not generate alpha. The carry is compensation for bearing the risk of a catastrophic volatility spike. This was dramatically demonstrated on February 5, 2018 ("Volmageddon"), when XIV lost 96% in a single session and was subsequently terminated, and in March 2020 when SVXY lost approximately 60%.

The strategy's appeal lies in its simplicity and historically attractive returns, but its risk profile — steady gains punctuated by catastrophic losses — requires rigorous position sizing and risk management to survive.

## Trading Rules

1. **Core Position**: Hold a short position in VXX (iPath Series B S&P 500 VIX Short-Term Futures ETN) or a long position in SVXY (ProShares Short VIX Short-Term Futures ETF, which provides -0.5x VIX short-term futures exposure).

2. **Entry Conditions**:
   - VIX futures term structure is in contango (VX2/VX1 ratio > 1.02).
   - VIX spot level is below 25 (calm regime).
   - If both conditions met, enter full position.

3. **Position Sizing**:
   - **Conservative**: Allocate 5-10% of portfolio to SVXY (equivalent to ~2.5-5% short VIX exposure).
   - **Moderate**: Allocate 10-20% of portfolio.
   - Never size such that a 100% loss on the volatility carry position would exceed 15% of total portfolio value.

4. **Volatility-Scaled Sizing**: Scale exposure inversely to VIX level:
   - VIX < 15: Full position.
   - VIX 15-20: 75% position.
   - VIX 20-25: 50% position.
   - VIX 25-30: 25% position.
   - VIX > 30: Flat (no position).

5. **Exit Rules**:
   - Close if VIX spikes above 30.
   - Close if term structure inverts to backwardation (VX2/VX1 < 0.98).
   - Close if SVXY drops more than 15% in a single day (momentum stop).

6. **Re-Entry**: After closing for risk management, re-enter only when VIX has returned below 22 AND term structure has re-entered contango for at least 5 consecutive trading days.

## Performance Metrics

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Sharpe Ratio | 0.8-1.3 (before tail events) |
| CAGR | 15-30% (period dependent, before tail losses) |
| Max Drawdown | -60% to -96% (February 2018, unmanaged) |
| Win Rate | 65-75% (monthly) |
| Volatility | 25-40% annualized |
| Profit Factor | 1.4-2.0 |
| Rebalancing | Daily (monitoring), signal-driven (entries/exits) |

The extreme range in reported performance reflects the difference between naive implementations (hold short vol continuously) and risk-managed versions. Naive XIV holding from 2011-2018 produced annualized returns above 50% with a Sharpe above 1.3 — until the terminal drawdown. Volatility-targeted versions with the VIX-based sizing rules above produce more modest returns (15-25% CAGR) but survivable drawdowns of 20-35%.

## Efficacy Rating

**Rating: 3/5** — The carry is real and economically grounded (compensation for bearing tail risk), but the strategy's risk-return profile is fundamentally asymmetric in the wrong direction for most investors. The 3/5 rating reflects: (a) the Volmageddon event proved that product-level risks can be catastrophic, (b) the strategy provides no alpha — only leveraged beta exposure to equities, (c) behavioral difficulty of maintaining positions through drawdowns, and (d) post-2018 regulatory and structural changes (SVXY reduced to 0.5x) have reduced the carry opportunity.

## Implementation Notes

- **Product Selection**: Post-2018, SVXY at -0.5x is the primary accessible instrument. Shorting VXX directly is an alternative but requires borrowing (which can be expensive or unavailable during stress) and faces unlimited theoretical loss. VIX futures provide the most direct exposure but require futures account approval and higher margin.
- **Path Dependency Warning**: Leveraged and inverse ETPs exhibit path dependency — their long-term returns diverge from the expected multiple of the underlying index due to daily rebalancing and volatility drag. This works in favor of short VIX products during contango (they decay slower than expected) but against them during choppy markets.
- **Issuer Risk**: ETNs (like VXX) are unsecured debt obligations of the issuer. If the issuer faces financial distress, the ETN can trade below its indicative value regardless of the underlying VIX exposure. ETFs (like SVXY) are preferable from a credit risk perspective.
- **Correlation to Equities**: Short vol carry has a correlation of approximately 0.7-0.8 to the S&P 500 (with ~4x effective beta). This means it provides minimal diversification benefit when added to an equity-heavy portfolio. It should be viewed as a leveraged equity substitute, not a diversifier.
- **Tax Treatment**: Short-term capital gains from frequent rebalancing are taxed at ordinary income rates in many jurisdictions. VIX futures receive favorable 60/40 long-term/short-term treatment under Section 1256 in the US.
